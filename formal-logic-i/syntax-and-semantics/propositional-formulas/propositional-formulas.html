What is a Propositional Formula

A propositional formula is a syntactic expression built from variables (such as \(p\) and \(q\)) using logical connectives like \(\neg\), \(\land\), \(\lor\), \(\rarrow\), and \(\ioi\). When specific truth values are assigned to all its variables, a propositional formula evaluates to a single, definite truth value of true or false.

More formally, a propositional formula is not itself a proposition but a formal expression that denotes a proposition, much like an algebraic expression (e.g., \( x + y \)) denotes a value but is not the value itself.

When the values of all variables in a propositional formula are specified, the formula determines a unique truth value. Thus, propositional formulas provide a systematic way to represent and analyse logical relationships between statements.

Writing Propositional Formulas

When writing formulas, the formal definition requires us to include many open and closed brackets. While these brackets are necessary for making the structure of a formula unambiguous, they can make formulas harder to read. For this reason, it is common practice to omit some brackets when writing formulas informally, as long as doing so does not create any ambiguity. In particular, we can always leave out the outermost brackets. However, it is important to recover the original formula by reinserting the brackets in accordance with the rules of precedence, which I will explain below.

  • The negation sign has the highest precedence. Just like how 'not' can turn the meaning of a whole sentence around, the negation sign modifies the truth value of the entire expression it applies to, overriding other operations.
  • The connectives \(\land\) and \(\lor\) have the second-highest precedence.
  • The connectives \(\rarrow\) and \(\sioi\) have the lowest precedence.
  • If we omit brackets between connectives that have the same level of precedence, we will associate them from right to left. For example, in the expression \(p \lor q \lor r\), we associate it as \( (p\lor (q \lor r))\).

The table below shows the difference between an a formally-written formula, and its informal abbreviation.

Informal Abbreviation Formal Formula
\(\neg p_1 \land p_2 \lor \neg p_3\) \(((\neg p_1) \land (p_2 \lor (\neg p_3)))\)
\(\neg p_1 \lor p_2 \rarrow p_3 \sioi p_4\) \((((\neg p_1) \lor p_2) \rarrow (p_3 \sioi p_4))\)

Question: Add brackets into the following to show their actual formulas:

  1. \(\neg p_1 \land p_2 \ioi p_3\)
  2. \(p_1 \lor p_2 \ioi p_3 \land \neg p_4\)
  3. \(p_1 \rarrow p_2 \sioi p_3 \land p_4\)

  1. Answer: \((((\neg p_1) \land p_2) \ioi p_3)\)

    Reason: \(\neg\) has the highest precedence, so you place its respective brackets first. \(\land\) has the next highest precedence, so you place its respective brackets next. And finally, \(\sioi\) has the lowest precedence, so you place its respective brackets last.

  2. Answer: \(((p_1 \lor p_2) \ioi (p_3 \land \neg p_4))\)

    Reason: \(\lor\) and \(\land\) have higher precedence than \(\sioi\), so their repsective brackets should be place first.

  3. Answer: \((p_1 \rarrow (p_2 \sioi (p_3 \land p_4)))\)

    Reason: \(\land\) has higher precedence than \(\sioi\) and \(\rarrow\), so its brackets are added first. \(\sioi\) and \(\rarrow\) have equal precedence, so their brackets are added from right to left.

Besides omitting brackets, it is also common to use variable names like \(p,\ q,\ r,\ \ldots\) instead of \(p_1,\ p_2,\ p_3,\ \ldots\) to make formulas easier to read. Formally, only variables of the form \(p_i\) are allowed in formulas, but using single-letter names is a helpful informal convention. The connectives \(\land,\ \lor\), and \(\sioi\) are associative, so the rule about associating from right to left does not affect their meaning. However, \(\rarrow\) is not associative: for example, \(p \rarrow (q \rarrow r)\) is not the same as \((p \rarrow q) \rarrow r\). On the other hand, \(p \rarrow (q \rarrow r)\) is truth-functionally equivalent to \(p \land q \rarrow r\), which is often a useful way to write formulas. This is one reason for choosing to associate from right to left. To avoid confusion, it is best not to write expressions like \(A \lor B \land C\) or \(A \rarrow B \sioi C\) without brackets. According to the conventions here, these mean \(A \lor (B \land C)\) and \(A \rarrow (B \sioi C)\), but other authors may use different conventions.
