Overview

In everyday conversations, the meaning of what we say can change depending on context and how we say it. For example, let me ask you a question: Why are you here? By emphasising the word 'here', I'm asking why you chose this website instead of another. Maybe I'm curious about what motivated you to visit or what you're hoping to find.

However, if I asked you, 'Why are You here!?', I'm now asking why you, in particular, are here, suggesting others are welcome, but you are not. This is much more personal and confrontational.

Even though both sentences use the same four words, their meanings are completely different. In maths, we need a way to avoid this kind of ambiguity and express our results clearly to avoid any misinterpretations. To do this, we use mathematical logic, where each symbol means only one thing and every statement follows strict rules to keep everything clear.

In the lessons below, we will first discuss the syntax and semantics of propositional logic, where we cover what a proposition is, how to correctly express it, and the definitions of some basic logical symbols. Afterwards, we will learn about the different methods for proving propositions.
