Common Notation and Syntax

Unfortunately, mathematics lacks a universal set of notation; symbols, syntax and semantics can have different meanings across textbooks, and some authors even use their own systems. To avoid ambiguity, I will list the notation, syntax, and semantics used throughout this website.

End of Proof Markers

In ancient Greek, the phrase ὅπερ ἔδει δεῖξαι (or ΟΕΔ) was commonly used by mathematicians such as Euclid and Archimedes to mark the end of a proof. During the European Renaissance, this was translated into Latin as quod erat demonstrandum (QED).

However, in modern mathematics, it is common to use the Halmos symbol (also known as the tombstone), represented as \(\blacksquare\). Following standard convention, the tombstone will be used here to indicate the end of a proof when appropriate.

Implied Multiplication

Multiplication can be expressed as explicit multiplication (such as \(2 \cdot 3\) or \(4 \times 6\)), or implicit multiplication (such as \(ab\)). Depending on the author, implicit multiplication may be parsed with different binding strength relative to other operations. Here are the two common conventions:

  1. High-precedence implicit multiplication.
    Implicit multiplication is treated as if it were surrounded by brackets, and therefore binds tighter than division.
  2. Equal-precedence implicit multiplication.
    Implicit multiplication is treated as having the same precedence as explicit multiplication.

It is important to know which convention an author is using, as the choice can significantly affect the interpretation of an expression. For example, take the following two in-line functions: \(1/ab\) and \(\cos ab\). If the the first convention is used, these are interpreted as:

  • \(\displaystyle 1 / ab = \frac{1}{ab} \)
  • \( \cos ab = \cos(ab) \)

However, if the second convention were used, the same expressions would instead be interpreted as:

  • \(\displaystyle 1 / ab = \frac{1}{a} \cdot b \)
  • \( \cos ab = \cos(a) \cdot b \)

Either convention is a stylistic choice of notation rather than a logical necessity. However, the second interpretation is less common in modern mathematical writing but does appear in some calculators and informal contexts. Throughout this website, the first convention is used: implicit multiplication binds more tightly than division. Whenever there is any possibility of ambiguity, brackets will be used to make the intended meaning explicit.
