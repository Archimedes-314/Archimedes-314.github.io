\section{Breaking Down the Question}
\paragraph{Question:} Solve \(\displaystyle \diff{x}{x}\)\\

While this may appear quite difficult at first glance, there is a hidden---yet simple---solution to solve it. After spending years on this question, I was amazed by its beauty once I had the answer.

Instead of tackling the problem as a standard derivative (these tend to be quite difficult), you can instead break it into fractional derivatives. While there are an infinite number of possibilities, splitting it into two half-derivatives produces the nicest solution.

Braking this into two half-derivatives can be solved with two different formulas. The first being as follows:
\[
    \ndiff{\alpha}{x^n}{x}=\frac{\Pi(n)}{\Pi(n-\alpha)}x^{n-\alpha}, \qquad \qquad \Pi(n)=\Gamma(n+1)=\Int{0}{\infty}{t^{n}e^{-t}}{t}
\]
This allows us to take the \(\alpha\)-order derivative of a function $f(x)=x^n$.

The second formula is a simple differintegral, and is given by:

\[
\EulerD{}{a}{\alpha}{t}f(t)=
    \left\{
        \begin{array}{cc}
            \displaystyle\ndiff{\beta}{}{t}\left[ \nfoldInt{\beta-\alpha}{a}{t}{f(x)}{x}\right], &  \alpha>0\\\displaystyle
            \nfoldInt{\modu{\alpha}}{a}{t}{f(x)}{x}, & \alpha<0
        \end{array}
    \right.
\]
Where \(\beta=\ceil{\alpha}\).
Applying the Riemann-Liouville fractional integral, we get the following for positive \(\alpha\):
\begin{align*}
    \EulerD{}{a}{\alpha}{t}f(t)&=\ndiff{\beta}{}{t}\left[\frac{1}{\Gamma(\beta-\alpha)}\Int{a}{t}{(t-s)^{\beta-\alpha-1}f(s)}{s}\right]\\
    \EulerD{}{a}{-\alpha}{t}f(t)&=\frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\Int{a}{t}{(t-s)^{\alpha-1}f(s)}{s}
\end{align*}

This means that we can write out the question as follows:
\begin{align*}
    \diff{x}{x}&=\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\ndiff{1/2}{x}{x}\right]\\
    &=\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\frac{\Pi(1)}{\Pi(1-\frac{1}{2})}x^{1-\frac{1}{2}}\right]\\
    &=\diff{}{x}\left[\frac{1}{\Gamma(1-\frac{1}{2})}\Int{a}{t}{(t-s)^{1-\frac{1}{2}-1}\left(
    \frac{\Pi(1)}{\Pi(1-\frac{1}{2})}s^{1-\frac{1}{2}}
    \right)}{s}\right]\\
    &=\diff{}{x}\left[\left(
    \Int{0}{\infty}{t^{-\frac{1}{2}}e^{-t}}{t}
    \right)^{-1}\Int{a}{t}{(t-s)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\left(
    \frac{\displaystyle
    \Int{0}{\infty}{te^{-t}}{t}
    }{\displaystyle
    \Int{0}{\infty}{t^{\frac{1}{2}}e^{-t}}{t}
    }\sqrt{s}
    \right)}{s}\right]
\end{align*}

\section{Solving the Question}
\subsection{Solving the Inner Half-Derivative}
Now that we have the necessary formulas, we can get started on the question.
\begin{align*}
    \diff{x}{x}&=\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\right]\\
    &=\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\frac{\Pi(1)}{\Pi\left(1-\frac{1}{2}\right)}x^{1-\frac{1}{2}}\right]\\
    &=\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\sqrt{x}\left(\Int{0}{\infty}{x^{\frac{1}{2}}e^{-x}}{x}\right)^{-1}\right]
\end{align*}
So now we will need to solve the integral, which is very simple. First, we need to use Integration by Parts.

\begin{align*}
    \Int{0}{\infty}{x^{\frac{1}{2}}e^{-x}}{x}&=\evalone{\Big.-e^{-x}\sqrt{x}}{0}{\infty}+\Int{0}{\infty}{\frac{1}{2e^x\sqrt{x}}}{x}\\
    \therefore \Int{0}{\infty}{x^{\frac{1}{2}}e^{-x}}{x}&=\Int{0}{\infty}{\frac{1}{2}x^{-\frac{1}{2}}e^{-x}}{x}
\end{align*}
Now we can let \(u=x^{\frac{1}{2}}\). Note that this is an even function.
\begin{align*}
    \Int{0}{\infty}{\frac{1}{2}x^{-\frac{1}{2}}e^{-x}}{x}&=\Int{0}{\infty}{e^{-u^2}}{u}\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}\lInt{\R}{e^{-u^2}}{u}
\end{align*}
Now we'll define this to be $I$, and do some simple manipulation.
\begin{align*}
    2I&=\lInt{\R}{e^{-x^2}}{x}\\
    4I^2&=\lInt{\R}{e^{-x^2}}{x}\cdot\lInt{\R}{e^{-y^2}}{y}\\
    &=\iint\limits_{\R\times\R}{e^{-x^2-y^2}}\d x\,\d y\\
    &=\lInt{\R^2}{e^{-\modu{z}^2}}{z}\\
    \mathcolor{gray}{\lIInt{D}{f(x,y)}{A}}&=\mathcolor{gray}{\lIInt{S}{f\Big(g(u,v),h(u,v)\Big)\modu{\begin{array}{cc}
        \pdiff{x}{u} & \pdiff{x}{v} \vspace{0.5em}\\
        \pdiff{y}{u} & \pdiff{y}{v}
    \end{array}}}{B}}\\
    &=\IInt{0}{2\pi}{\R_0^+}{}{e^{-r^2}\modu{\begin{array}{cc}\displaystyle
        \pdiff{r\cos\phi}{r} &\displaystyle \pdiff{r\cos\phi}{\phi} \vspace{0.5em}\\\displaystyle
        \pdiff{r\sin\phi}{r} &\displaystyle \pdiff{r\sin\phi}{\phi}
    \end{array}}}{r}{\phi}\\
    &=\IInt{0}{2\pi}{\R_0^+}{}{e^{-r^2}\modu{\begin{array}{cc}
        \cos\phi & -r\sin\phi \\
        \sin\phi & r\cos\phi
    \end{array}}}{r}{\phi}\\
    &=\Int{\R_0^+}{}{\Int{0}{2\pi}{re^{-r^2}}{\phi}}{r}\\
    &=\pi\Int{\R_0^+}{}{2re^{-r^2}}{r}\\
    4I^2&=\pi\\
    \therefore \ I&=\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}
\end{align*}
Substituting this back in gives:
\begin{align*}
    \diff{x}{x}&=\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\sqrt{x}\left(\Int{0}{\infty}{x^{\frac{1}{2}}e^{-x}}{x}\right)^{-1}\right]\\
    &=\ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\frac{2\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{\pi}}\right]
\end{align*}

\subsection{Solving the Outer Half-Derivative}
Now we will take our result from section 3.1 and substitute it into our Riemann-Liouville integral.

\begin{align*}
    \ndiff{1/2}{}{x}\left[\frac{2\sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{\pi}}\right] &= \diff{}{x}\left[ \frac{1}{\Gamma\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)} \Int{0}{x}{(x-t)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\frac{2\sqrt{t}}{\sqrt{\pi}}}{t}\right]\\
    &=\frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}\Gamma{\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)}}\cdot\diff{}{x}\left[ \Int{0}{x}{\frac{\sqrt{t}}{\sqrt{x-t}}}{t} \right]
\end{align*}

Now we'll solve the integral. We'll perform two substitutions. The first being \(u=\sqrt{t}, \ \d t=2u\ \d u\). This gives the following:
\[
    \Int{}{}{\frac{\sqrt{t}}{\sqrt{x-t}}}{t}=2\Int{}{}{\frac{u^2}{\sqrt{x-u^2}}}{u}
\]

Now we'll let \(u=\sqrt{x}\sin{v}, \ \d u =\sqrt{x}\cos{v} \ \d v\).
\begin{align*}
    2\Int{}{}{\frac{u^2}{\sqrt{x-u^2}}}{u}&=2\Int{}{}{\frac{
    x\sqrt{x}\cos{v}\sin^2{v}
    }{
    \sqrt{x-x\sin^2{v}}
    }}{v}\\
    &=2x\Int{}{}{\sin^2{v}}{v}
\end{align*}

This is where knowing your reduction formulas comes in handy for you. The reduction formula for $\Int{}{}{\sin^n{x}}{x}$ is as follows:

\[
    \Int{}{}{\sin^n{x}}{x}=\frac{n-1}{n}\Int{}{}{\sin^{n-2}x}{x}-\frac{\sin^{n-1}x\cos{x}}{n}
\]
Now using that and undoing our substitutions, we get:
\begin{align*}
    2\Int{}{}{\frac{u^2}{\sqrt{x-u^2}}}{u}&=2x\Int{}{}{\sin^2{v}}{v}\\
    &=xv-x\sin{v}\cos{v}\\
    &=x\sin^{-1}\left( \frac{u}{\sqrt{x}} \right)-x\sin\left( \sin^{-1}\left( \frac{u}{\sqrt{x}} \right) \right)\cos\left({\sin^{-1}\left( \frac{u}{\sqrt{x}} \right)}\right)\\
    &=x\sin^{-1}\left( \frac{u}{\sqrt{x}} \right)-\frac{xu}{\sqrt{x}}\sqrt{1-\frac{u^2}{x}}\\
    &=x\sin^{-1}\left( \sqrt{\frac{t}{x}} \right) -x\sqrt{\frac{t}{x}}\sqrt{1-\frac{t}{x}}\\
    \implies \ \Int{0}{x}{\sqrt{\frac{t}{x-t}}}{t}&=\evalone{x\sin^{-1}\left( \sqrt{\frac{t}{x}} \right) -x\sqrt{\frac{t}{x}}\sqrt{1-\frac{t}{x}}}{0}{x}\\
    &=x\sin^{-1}{1}\\
    &=\frac{x\pi}{2}\\
    \therefore \ \diff{x}{x}&=\frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}\Gamma{(\frac{1}{2})}}\cdot\diff{}{x}\left(\frac{x\pi}{2}\right)\\
    &=1
\end{align*}
