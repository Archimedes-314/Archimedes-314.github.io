Overview

Why Formal Mathematics is Needed

In everyday life, we often use maths without thinking twice, whether it's basic arithmetic in cooking, or algebra in budgeting. But as we move from everyday maths to more advanced topics such as calculus and analysis, certain ideas or definitions we used before aren't as precise or rigorous as we once thought.

For example, consider this familiar question: Is \(1 = 0.\bar{9}\)?

This is a common internet problem that often generates plenty of controversy. A common argument for why it's true relies on the idea that the real numbers are dense, which means that for any two real numbers, there must exist another real number between them. This property seems obvious enough: for any two numbers \(a,b\in\R\), we must have \(a\lt (a + b)/2\lt b\).

Using this fact, we could show that there exists no real number \(x\) such that \(0.\bar{9} \lt x \lt 1\). However, this argument relies on two assumptions: that the reals are dense (as stated), and that \(0.\bar{9}\) is a valid real number.

Thus, to prove \(1 = 0.\bar{9}\) through this method, we first need to prove our two assumptions. But this raises a bigger question: How far back do we need to go to justify these assumptions; is there a point where our claims gain objective validity?

How We Formalise Mathematics

This is where the story of formal and rigorous mathematics begins.

Since the late 19th century, mathematicians have been fascinated with formalising mathematics. Alfred Whitehead and Bertrand Russell explored this topic when writing their book Principia Mathematica, where they attempted to remove all assumptions used in the current construction of the real number system.
However, in the 1930s, Kurt Gödel and his incompleteness theorems showed that this project is fundamentally impossible; any system complex enough to do basic arithmetic will include assumptions that can not be proven within the system itself. In essence, no mathematical system can be both complete (able to prove every true statement) and consistent (free from contradictions).

This leads us to an important question: If no system can be complete and consistent, how can we build a rigorous framework for mathematics? The answer lies in axioms. Axioms are basic, self-evident statements derived from our natural understanding of numbers. While axioms are not provable within the system itself, they are accepted as starting points that allow us to develop more complex and reliable mathematical structures.

In the next sections, we will explore the axioms of the real number system. These axioms allow us to build the rigorous mathematical theories that underpin calculus, real analysis, and beyond.
