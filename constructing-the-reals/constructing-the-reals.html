Understanding The Construction of Real Numbers through the Axioms

Overview

Axioms are the foundation of formal mathematics, as they provide a rigid structure for how the real numbers behave. To demonstrate why these axioms are necessary, consider a simple property like commutativity which states for any two numbers \(a,b\in\R, \ a+b=b+a\). This property is very intuitive for the natural numbers. For example, if you have \(3\) apples and then get \(5\) more, you end up with \(8\) apples, just like if you had started with \(5\) apples and got \(3\) more.

But what happens when you move beyond the natural numbers to more abstract numbers like the irrationals? If you have two irrational numbers like \(\e\) and \(\pi\), how would you show \(\e+\pi=\pi+\e\)? These numbers don't have a clear representation on the numberline like the naturals, and it's not immedeatly obvious why the equality should hold.

This is where axioms can be used to define the properties of real numbers. The natural numbers are the best place to begin due to the intuitive nature of their properties. After understanding why the natural numbers behave the way they do, we can then define the integers through the naturals. This allows us to carry the self-evident properties of the naturals to the definition of the integers. Then we can define the rationals through the integers, tying their definition to the naturals. We can then do the same with the real numbers.
