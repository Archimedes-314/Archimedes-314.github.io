Purpose and Scope

This website explores how mathematical logic can be used to rigorously construct the real numbers and understand their properties. We begin with the basics of logic: learning what symbols mean, how to interpret statements, and how to use logical reasoning to prove mathematical concepts. Topics include propositional, first-order, and second-order logic.

Using this foundation, we develop axiomatic systems to define the integers and rational numbers from the natural numbers, and then construct the irrational numbers and the real numbers. Along the way, we analyse fundamental properties of the real number system, including completeness, density, and incompleteness phenomena.

Once we have a solid understanding of the real numbers, we return to logic, focusing on syntax, semantics, and mathematical conventions. For example, we examine why implicit multiplication is often given precedence over division, how such conventions can be formally defined, and what it means for them to be well-defined and consistent.

In the future, this website will expand to include lessons on linear algebra, calculus, and other areas of mathematics, always with an emphasis on rigorous logical foundations.

Common Notation and Syntax

Unfortunately, mathematics lacks a universal set of notation; symbols, syntax and semantics can have different meanings across textbooks, and some authors even use their own systems. End of Proof Markers

In ancient Greek, the phrase ὅπερ ἔδει δεῖξαι (or ΟΕΔ) was commonly used by mathematicians such as Euclid and Archimedes to mark the end of a proof. During the European Renaissance, this was translated into Latin as quod erat demonstrandum (Q.E.D.).

However, in modern mathematics, it is common to use the Halmos symbol (also known as the tombstone), represented as \(\blacksquare\). Following standard convention, the tombstone will be used here to indicate the end of a proof when appropriate.

Implied Multiplication

Multiplication can be shown in two ways: with a symbol (such as \(2 \cdot 3\) or \(4 \times 6\)), or without one, by writing values together (such as \(ab\), which means \(a\) times \(b\)). When a multiplication symbol is used, the operation is called explicit multiplication. When no symbol is used, it is called implicit (or implied) multiplication. You may also see this referred to as multiplication by juxtaposition.

Depending on the author, implicit multiplication may be parsed with different binding strength relative to other operations. Here are the two common conventions:

  1. High-precedence implicit multiplication.
    Implicit multiplication is treated as if it were surrounded by brackets, and therefore binds more tightly than division.
  2. Equal-precedence implicit multiplication.
    Implicit multiplication is treated as having the same precedence as explicit multiplication.

It is important to understand which convention an author is using, as the choice can significantly affect the interpretation of an expression. For example, under the first convention, implicit multiplication always takes precedence over division, and functions written without brackets are interpreted as having all immediately following implicitly multiplied values as part of the function's argument:

  • \( 1 / ab = \frac{1}{ab} \)
  • \( \cos ab = \cos(ab) \)

However, if the second convention were used, the same expressions would instead be interpreted as:

  • \( 1 / ab = \frac{1}{a} \cdot b \)
  • \( \cos ab = \cos(a) \cdot b \)

Either convention is a stylistic choice of notation rather than a logical necessity. However, the second interpretation is less common in modern mathematical writing but does appear in some calculators and informal contexts. Throughout this website, the first convention is used: implicit multiplication binds more tightly than division. Whenever there is any possibility of ambiguity, brackets will be used to make the intended meaning explicit.
